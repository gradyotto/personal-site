# Why American Dynamism Needs AI

The case for deploying frontier AI models not just in software, but on the factory floor—where the real leverage lives.

## The Software Trap

Most AI discourse centers on chatbots, code generation, and digital assistants. These are real and valuable applications. But they represent a fraction of the economic surface area where AI could create leverage.

The United States still manufactures $2.3 trillion worth of goods annually. The factories producing those goods run on decades-old control systems, tribal knowledge locked in retiring workers' heads, and planning tools that haven't fundamentally changed since the 1990s.

This is where AI should be deployed most aggressively.

## Why the Factory Floor Matters

Consider a typical CNC machining operation. A skilled operator watches chip formation, listens to spindle sounds, and adjusts feed rates based on intuition built over years. That intuition is a model—a remarkably good one—but it doesn't scale, it retires, and it can't be replicated.

Modern sensor arrays can capture everything that operator perceives and more: vibration signatures, thermal profiles, acoustic emissions, power draw curves. The data is there. What's missing is the intelligence layer that turns sensor streams into decisions.

## The Reshoring Multiplier

Here's the strategic argument: if AI can make a U.S. factory 30% more productive, it doesn't just improve margins. It changes the calculus on where to build things. Labor cost differentials shrink when your domestic workforce is augmented by systems that handle the cognitive overhead of complex manufacturing.

This isn't about replacing workers. It's about making each worker's judgment reach further.

## What Needs to Happen

Three things need to converge:

1. **Hardware-aware AI models** that understand physical constraints, not just token sequences
2. **Edge deployment infrastructure** that runs inference at machine-cycle speeds
3. **Domain translators**—people who speak both ML and manufacturing—to bridge the gap

The last point is the bottleneck. We have plenty of ML engineers. We have plenty of machinists. We have almost nobody who is both.

## The Opportunity

The companies that figure out this translation layer—between bits and atoms, between neural networks and ball screws—will define the next era of American manufacturing. The opportunity isn't incremental. It's structural.

And it's wide open.
