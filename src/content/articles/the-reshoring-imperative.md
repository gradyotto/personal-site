# The Reshoring Imperative

Supply chain fragility isn't a theory—it's a quarterly earnings risk. How small manufacturers can lead the reshoring charge.

## The Wake-Up Call

The past few years delivered a masterclass in supply chain fragility. Factories idled for want of a single component. Lead times stretched from weeks to months. Companies discovered that their "lean" supply chains were actually "brittle" supply chains optimized for a world without disruption.

The lesson was expensive but clear: geographic concentration of manufacturing capacity is a risk, not just a cost optimization.

## Why Reshoring Is Hard

The economics of offshoring didn't happen by accident. Decades of investment created manufacturing ecosystems abroad—clusters of suppliers, skilled workforces, and institutional knowledge—that can't be replicated overnight.

Reshoring isn't just about building factories. It's about rebuilding capability. And capability is a function of people, processes, and accumulated know-how that takes years to develop.

## The Small Manufacturer Advantage

Here's what most reshoring discourse misses: the path back doesn't run through megafactories. It runs through small and medium manufacturers—shops with 20 to 200 employees—that can be nimble, specialized, and responsive in ways that large operations can't.

These shops are the backbone of American manufacturing. They make the components that go into everything from medical devices to defense systems. And they're uniquely positioned to absorb reshored demand if given the right tools and support.

## What Small Shops Need

Three things would accelerate reshoring through small manufacturers:

1. **Technology that reduces the skill gap.** Modern manufacturing requires expertise that's in short supply. AI-assisted programming, automated inspection, and intelligent scheduling can help smaller teams punch above their weight.

2. **Access to demand signals.** Small shops often operate in information deserts. They don't know what contracts are available, what capabilities are needed, or where the demand is flowing. Better market infrastructure would help.

3. **Capital efficiency tools.** A shop that can quote faster, schedule smarter, and utilize machines more fully can compete on cost without competing on wages.

## The Strategic Argument

This isn't just an economic argument. It's a strategic one. A country that can't make things is a country that depends on others for its critical needs. And dependence, in a world of geopolitical competition, is vulnerability.

Reshoring won't happen through policy alone. It'll happen when the economics work—when technology makes domestic production competitive, when small manufacturers have the tools to scale, and when companies realize that resilience has a return on investment.

The imperative is clear. The question is whether we'll move fast enough.
